About

Hunter Cobbs

Senior Solutions Architect @ Forescout Technologies

Kansas City, Missouri

Yes, that's a cartoon. No, I don't actually look that put-together before my third cup of coffee.

I've spent the better part of two decades convincing computers to do things they really didn't want to do. Mostly in Linux, where the documentation is "read the source code" and the error messages are written by people who assume you already know what went wrong.

At Forescout, I architect cybersecurity solutions focused on one core problem: figuring out what's actually connected to your network. I design and deploy systems that discover every device, classify what it is, determine if it belongs there, and remove it if it doesn't. The work covers pre-sales architecture, implementation, and troubleshooting. I also handle integrating Forescout with whatever other security tools are already in place. Most organizations are surprised by what's actually on their networks. And a lot of it has no business being there.

Patents

I hold two U.S. patents, which sounds impressive until you realize they're about making embedded devices fail gracefully. Turns out, teaching hardware to recover from its own mistakes is a patentable innovation. Who knew.

US8645772B2 System and method for upgradable remotely deployed intelligent communication devices

Boot failure detection and image restoration. Because devices will find creative ways to brick themselves, and someone has to plan for that.

US8694824B2 System and method for managing uncertain events for communication devices

Network connection monitoring and recovery. Networks are unreliable. This patent is about accepting that reality and dealing with it like adults.

The Technical Bits

My background spans embedded systems, firmware development, and cybersecurity architecture. I've written code that runs on devices with less memory than this webpage consumes, and I've architected security solutions for networks with more endpoints than some countries have people.

Linux is my natural habitat. I've compiled kernels for fun, debugged driver issues at 2 AM for less fun, and have strong opinions about init systems that I'll share whether you ask or not.

Current Obsession

These days, I'm exploring how AI can augment software development without producing the kind of code that makes future maintainers question your life choices. Hence this portfolio, where every commit is labeled so you can see exactly who (or what) is responsible for each brilliant decision and inevitable bug.

The verdict so far: AI is a surprisingly capable pair programmer, provided you actually pay attention. If you don't, you'll get code that technically works but spiritually shouldn't exist.
